What's covered
Trak's team layer has four parts: your people (a dedicated members page, workspace roles, and named teams), task ownership (assignees, priorities, due dates, and deadlines that surface everywhere), structured work (task blocks, timelines, and project canvases that make work visible), and how the team communicates (inline comments, mentions, and a Slack bot connected to the same data).
Your people

Set up your workspace once — everyone knows what they can touch

👥
A dedicated members page
Every workspace has a Members page where you can see everyone on the team, their role, and manage access in one place. No digging through settings — it's always a click away.
🔑
Workspace roles and project-level permissions
Assign roles at the workspace level (owner, admin, member). Then go further — configure access per project so that, say, your creative agency can only see the Campaign project and nothing else. Permissions are both broad and surgical.
🏷️
Teams — groups you assign and configure together
Create named teams (Creative, Growth, Ops) and add members to them. You can assign tasks, configure project access, and manage permissions at the team level — so adding a new designer to the Creative team gives them the right access immediately.
Why it matters: D2C brand teams wear a lot of hats. Someone might be creative lead on one project and a reviewer on another. Teams + project permissions means the right people see the right work without anyone getting lost in someone else's backlog.

Ownership

Assign ownership to anything — not just tasks

👤
Assignees are a universal property
In most tools, only tasks have owners. In Trak, any block can have an assignee — a task, a timeline milestone, a file review, a gallery, a section of work. If something needs to get done, someone can own it, regardless of what kind of block it is.
✅
Priority and due dates travel with ownership
Assignees come paired with due dates and priority levels — urgent, high, medium, low — on every block that supports them. These aren't decorative. They're what the dashboard uses to surface what's overdue, due today, and due soon across your whole workspace.
🔍
Nothing without an owner falls through the cracks
Because ownership isn't limited to a task list, your team can assign the shoot brief, the asset gallery, the timeline event, and the launch checklist — and have all of it surface in the right dashboards and overviews automatically.
Think of it this way: a task is one type of work. But a shoot block, a mood board, a copy doc, a timeline event — those are work too. In Trak, any of them can have an owner, a deadline, and a priority. Your team's accountability layer isn't confined to a checklist.

Team visibility

Ask Trak who's carrying what — scoped exactly to what you need to know

Once your team has ownership across work, you can ask Trak to surface it as a chart — by person, by project, by priority, by status. No setup, no exports. Just describe the question and the answer appears.

💬
Ask in plain language
Type "who has overdue work on Summer Drop?" or "show me the task load across the Creative team this week" and Trak generates the chart from your live workspace data. No filters to configure, no spreadsheet to export to.
🎯
Infinite scope — one project or the whole workspace
Narrow to a single project, a specific team, a priority level, a date range, or any combination. Who on Growth has urgent tasks right now? How is work distributed across everyone on Brand Refresh? The conditions are yours — Trak handles the rest.
📌
Pin it wherever it's most useful
The chart drops in as a block — keep it on the dashboard for a persistent team view, or put it inside a project tab so workload visibility lives right next to the work itself.
This is what ownership data is for. Assigning work across your team is only half of it — being able to instantly see where things are piling up, who's overloaded, and what's slipping is what actually lets you manage people well.

Structured work

Tabs and timelines that give every piece of work a place to live

📑
Tabs organize work into areas, not lists
A project can have a Strategy tab, a Campaign tab, a Shopify tab, a Shoot tab — each with its own blocks, tasks, and files. Subtabs let you go deeper. The structure matches how your team actually thinks about the work, not how a generic PM tool wants you to.
📅
Timeline blocks for sequences and milestones
Add a Timeline block to any tab to map out work over time — with start and end dates, assignees, and priority. Timeline events sync to the workspace calendar so nothing lives only in one place.
🤖
AI can build the structure for you
Open a Workflow page, describe what you're working on, and the AI panel can generate a block layout — tabs, task lists, timelines, and sections — based on what your project needs. Start from scratch in seconds rather than building it out manually.

How the team communicates

Comments and mentions that keep feedback attached to the work

💬
Comment on any block, not the project in general
Every block in Trak has its own comment thread — so feedback on a hero image lives on the hero image block, not in a general channel or a comment buried in a doc. Threaded replies keep conversations focused.
@
@ mentions that link to specific items
While commenting, use @ to mention a teammate or reference another task or block directly. The mention appears inline in the comment — one click takes you there. No more "which file are you referring to?"
📬
Comment feeds on the dashboard and project overview
Recent team comments appear both on the workspace dashboard and in every project's Overview tab. Before a standup, open the project overview — you're caught up in 20 seconds without opening a single tab.
Client comments are separate. If you've shared a Magic Link with a client, their feedback comes in labeled as external — your team always knows what's internal discussion and what's client feedback, in the same view.

Where your team already lives

The Trak Slack bot — update work without opening a new tab

Your team is already in Slack. The Trak bot connects directly to your workspace data — tasks, projects, and AI — so the team can act on work without breaking their flow.

Ask it anything about your workspace
Ping the bot with a question — "what's overdue on the Summer Drop?" or "who owns the Shopify setup task?" — and it pulls the answer directly from Trak. No context switching.
Create and update tasks from Slack
The bot can create new tasks, mark tasks complete, reassign them, and update priorities — all from a message. When someone says "can you take that off my plate" in a thread, act on it immediately.
AI-powered, not just a slash command
The bot is connected to Trak's AI — so it understands context. Ask it to "summarize what's happening on the brand refresh" and it gives you a narrative answer, not a raw data dump.

Access control

The right people see the right projects — and nothing they shouldn't

As your brand team grows — freelancers, agency partners, seasonal hires — you need access control that's both easy to set up and precise enough to matter.

🔒
Per-project permissions for individuals or teams
Lock down specific projects so only certain members — or entire teams — can view or edit them. Your agency creative team sees the Campaign project. Your ops lead sees everything. Freelancers see only what they're working on.
⚡
Add a team, not a list of people
Because teams are groups, granting the Creative team access to a project means everyone in that group gets it — and anyone you add to that team later gets it automatically. One configuration, no maintenance.
Magic Links work alongside permissions. If you need a client or external stakeholder to see specific project tabs, share a Magic Link — they see only what you expose, without needing a workspace account at all.
